01
★ Oral   RL Scaling · LLMs

The Art of Scaling Reinforcement Learning Compute for LLMs

Devvrit, Madaan, Tiwari, Bansal, Duvvuri, Zaheer, Dhillon, Brandfonbrener, Agarwal

A 400,000 GPU-hour systematic study fitting sigmoidal compute-performance curves to RL training on LLMs. The headline finding: design choices like loss aggregation, normalization, curriculum, and off-policy algorithm mostly affect compute efficiency, not the asymptote — while a few choices genuinely move the ceiling. They distill the lessons into ScaleRL, a recipe whose 100k-GPU-hour run was successfully predicted from smaller-scale fits.

Why it matters
RL on LLMs has lacked the scaling laws that pre-training enjoys. This paper provides the first principled framework for extrapolating from small runs — turning algorithmic decisions into testable predictions.

02
Oral   ·   RLVR · Trust Regions

TROLL: Trust Regions Improve Reinforcement Learning for Large Language Models

Becker, Freymuth, Thilges, Otto, Neumann  ·  KIT, Tübingen

Argues that PPO's clipping objective is a crude stand-in for the KL trust region it was meant to approximate, and replaces it with a discrete differentiable trust region projection over the model's most important token logits. On math reasoning and code generation, TROLL beats PPO-style clipping on training speed, stability, and final success rate — without changing inference behavior.

Why it matters
Everyone uses PPO clipping; few revisit it. This is a drop-in replacement that recovers the theoretical motivation everyone quietly abandoned.

03
Oral   ·   Zero-Shot RL · Foundation Models

Optimistic Task Inference for Behavior Foundation Models

Rupf, Bagatella, Vlastelica, Krause  ·  ETH Zürich

Behavior Foundation Models retrieve a policy for any reward at test-time, but typically need rewards labeled across a large inference dataset. OpTI-BFM instead infers the task purely through environment interaction: an optimistic criterion that models uncertainty over reward functions and guides exploration. With a regret bound borrowed from linear bandit theory, BFMs identify and optimize an unseen reward in a handful of episodes.

Why it matters
Removes the last labeling bottleneck for zero-shot RL: you no longer need a reward function or a labeled inference set — just the ability to act in the environment.

04
Oral   ·   Unsupervised RL · Representations

TD-JEPA: Latent-Predictive Representations for Zero-Shot RL

Bagatella, Pirotta, Touati, Lazaric, Tirinzoni  ·  Meta FAIR, ETH Zürich

Extends JEPA-style self-prediction to RL by using temporal difference learning to predict long-term latent dynamics across multiple policies — from offline, reward-free transitions. Trains state and task encoders plus a policy-conditioned multi-step predictor entirely in latent space, enabling zero-shot reward optimization. Matches or beats SOTA on 13 ExoRL/OGBench datasets, especially in zero-shot RL from pixels.

Why it matters
Demonstrates that successor-feature-style transfer can be learned implicitly through latent prediction — with theory showing the predictor recovers them automatically.

05
Oral   ·   Exploration · Diffusion Policies

Exploratory Diffusion Model for Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning

Ying, Chen, Zhou, Hao, Su, Zhu  ·  Tsinghua

ExDM uses diffusion models to fit the heterogeneous replay-buffer distributions that plague unsupervised RL, then derives a score-based intrinsic reward that pushes the agent into under-visited regions. Comes with theory and practical algorithms for fine-tuning diffusion policies under limited interaction budgets. New SOTA on Maze2d and URLB, particularly strong in cross-embodiment settings.

Why it matters
Intrinsic motivation has been stuck on simple density models for a decade. Diffusion offers a far more expressive estimator — and the score function falls out for free.

06
Oral   ·   LLM Agents · Active Reasoning

Reducing Belief Deviation in RL for Active Reasoning of LLM Agents

Zou, Chen, Wang, Yang, Li, Da, Cheng, Li, Gong  ·  CUHK, Georgia Tech

LLM agents doing multi-turn information gathering suffer belief deviation: their internal model of the problem drifts from the true state, after which trajectories degenerate into uninformative loops — poisoning the RL credit signal. T³ detects this drift and truncates training trajectories at the deviation point, preserving credit for informative prefixes. Up to +30 points on agent benchmarks while cutting token cost by 34%.

Why it matters
A practical fix for one of the most frustrating failure modes in agent RL — and a reminder that classical RL diagnostics (credit assignment, trajectory quality) still pay off in the LLM era.
